Picturing Energy Efficiency

How to describe two-hundred methods of saving energy without words

When we decided to build a new design system for Opower (check out this page for a deep-dive on that process), we made some bold decisions about imagery. Specifically, we decided that stock photography had to disappear from all of our products for a few different reasons:

  1. Stock photography didn’t fit with the rest of the design system.
  2. We always had to settle when choosing a photograph: it was rare that we ever found the perfect photo that nailed the concept.
  3. Photographs were too locale-specific. Since Opower went international, we needed a library of images that worked for a multitude of countries, ethnic groups, and cultures.
  4. Stock photos? Gross.

Out with photos! In with custom illustrations.

We use images in a few different ways across Opower’s web, email, and print products. The most common use, however, was instructional: to demonstrate energy-efficient behaviors or the outcomes of saving energy. Most of our products make use of an expansive library of instructional tips—how-to guides for becoming more efficient—and every tip had a descriptive photograph.

To help us define a unique illustration style to replace these photographs, we brought in visual designer Amanda Dowd. The style had to be simple and friendly, and also had to translate well across languages and locales.

While Amanda nailed down our style, we settled on some rules for our new illustration system:

No people
We could convey human presence or activity, but we should never depict actual humans.

Simple color
Keep the colors in a neutral palette of grays, except for one color that would pop and draw attention to the energy-efficient behavior (this color would be customizable to match our customers’ brands).

Keep it fun
Every so often, throw in a cat. Or a dog, or bird, or any fun quirky thing to make our products fun, family-friendly, and interesting.

Two sizes
Every illustration should come in two levels of detail: a more detailed version to blow up big, and a smaller, less-detailed version for thumbnails and decorative elements.

With our style designed and our framework established, we started churning out illustrations. We only had a few hundred to do! The designers who contributed to this awesome library include Amanda Dowd, Arthur Mount, Ryan Hansz, myself, and many others.
